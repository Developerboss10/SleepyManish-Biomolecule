Biomolecules: The Building Blocks of Life

1. Introduction to Biomolecules

Biomolecules are organic molecules essential for life. They include carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids. These molecules are synthesized in living organisms and perform various biological functions.

2. Carbohydrates: Energy Providers

Definition: Carbohydrates are organic compounds made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in a 1:2:1 ratio.

Types of Carbohydrates:

  • Monosaccharides (Simple sugars): Glucose, Fructose
  • Disaccharides (Two monosaccharides joined): Maltose, Sucrose
  • Polysaccharides (Long chains): Starch, Cellulose, Glycogen

Functions: Primary energy source (e.g., glucose), Structural component (e.g., cellulose in plants).

Interesting Facts: Sucrose is a non-reducing sugar, while maltose and lactose are reducing sugars. Glycogen is the energy storage molecule in animals.

3. Proteins: The Workhorses of Cells

Definition: Proteins are polymers of amino acids joined by peptide bonds.

Structure of Proteins:

  • Primary structure: Sequence of amino acids.
  • Secondary structure: Alpha-helix or beta-pleated sheet formed by hydrogen bonding.
  • Tertiary structure: 3D folding stabilized by hydrogen bonds, disulfide bonds, and hydrophobic interactions.
  • Quaternary structure: Multiple polypeptides joined together (e.g., hemoglobin).

Functions: Enzymes (e.g., amylase for starch breakdown), Transport (e.g., hemoglobin carries oxygen), Structural (e.g., keratin in hair).

Essential Amino Acids: Humans must obtain essential amino acids like methionine from their diet.

4. Lipids: The Energy Reserves

Definition: Lipids are hydrophobic molecules, including fats, oils, and steroids.

Types of Lipids:

  • Triglycerides: Fats stored in adipose tissue.
  • Phospholipids: Major components of cell membranes.
  • Steroids: Hormones like cholesterol.

Functions: Long-term energy storage; Structural components of membranes.

Fun Fact: Phospholipids form the bilayer of cell membranes, with hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails.

5. Nucleic Acids: The Blueprint of Life

Definition: Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides that store and transfer genetic information.

Types of Nucleic Acids:

  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): Double-helix structure discovered by Watson and Crick. Contains adenine (A), thymine (T), cytosine (C), and guanine (G). Adenine pairs with thymine, and cytosine pairs with guanine.
  • RNA (Ribonucleic Acid): Single-stranded molecule involved in protein synthesis. Contains adenine, uracil (U), cytosine, and guanine.

Functions: DNA: Stores genetic information; RNA: Helps in protein synthesis.

6. Enzymes: The Biological Catalysts

Enzymes are proteins that speed up biochemical reactions without being consumed. Example: Amylase breaks down starch into maltose.

Properties of Enzymes: Highly specific to their substrate; Function is influenced by temperature and pH.

7. Vitamins: Essential Micronutrients

Vitamins are organic compounds required in small amounts for metabolic functions. Fat-soluble vitamins: A, D, E, and K. Water-soluble vitamins: B-complex and C.

Example: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) boosts immunity; Vitamin D aids calcium absorption.

8. Glycolysis: The Energy Cycle

Glycolysis is the breakdown of glucose into pyruvate, producing ATP. The first step is catalyzed by hexokinase, which converts glucose to glucose-6-phosphate.
